\begin{abstract}
General Relativity (GR) and Quantum Mechanics (QM) remain mathematically incompatible despite a century of effort. This paper argues that the failure of unification arises from a fundamental structural asymmetry: gravity is not a force within spacetime but the geometric framework that \emph{defines} spacetime. All particles and fields exist within this gravitational structure, making gravity universal in a way no quantum interaction is. Because quantum field theory requires a fixed background while GR denies the existence of such a background, unification cannot proceed by treating gravity as another quantum field. A successful theory must instead emerge from a deeper, pre-geometric quantum substrate from which both spacetime and quantum fields arise. This conceptual insight is formalized here as the \emph{Gravitational Baseline Principle}.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Einstein's General Relativity (GR) revolutionized physics by identifying gravity with the curvature of spacetime itself \cite{einstein1916}. Quantum Mechanics (QM), developed in parallel, describes matter and radiation through probabilistic amplitudes evolving on a fixed spacetime background \cite{dirac1930}. Despite their empirical success, the two theories remain conceptually incompatible.

This paper develops a conceptual argument: GR and QM cannot be unified as long as gravity is treated as a field analogous to the electromagnetic, weak, or strong interactions. Gravity is not a field \emph{within} spacetime; it is the structure that \emph{defines} spacetime. This asymmetry prevents unification at the level of fields and suggests that a deeper, pre-geometric quantum substrate must underlie both theories.

\section{Gravity as Universal Geometry}

Einstein's field equations,
\begin{equation}
G_{\mu\nu} = 8\pi G\, T_{\mu\nu},
\end{equation}
express the idea that matter and energy determine the curvature of spacetime. Every particle, regardless of charge or composition, follows geodesics determined by this curvature. Gravity couples to all forms of energy and momentum.

By contrast, the quantum interactions are selective:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Electromagnetism acts only on electrically charged particles.
    \item The weak interaction acts only on left-handed fermions.
    \item The strong interaction acts only on quarks and gluons.
\end{itemize}

Gravity alone is universal. It is not one interaction among many; it is the geometric background in which all interactions occur.

\section{The Asymmetry Problem}

Quantum field theory (QFT) assumes a fixed spacetime background. GR denies the existence of such a background. This leads to a structural contradiction:

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{|c|c|}
\hline
\textbf{Quantum Mechanics} & \textbf{General Relativity} \\
\hline
Requires fixed spacetime & Spacetime is dynamic \\
Fields evolve \emph{in} spacetime & Geometry \emph{is} the field \\
Linear operators & Nonlinear curvature \\
Local interactions & Background-dependent \\
\hline
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

This mismatch is conceptual, not technical. One theory requires a stage; the other \emph{is} the stage.

\section{The Gravitational Baseline Principle}

\textbf{Principle (Gravitational Baseline Principle).}  
\emph{Gravity is not a force within spacetime but the geometric structure that defines spacetime. Because all physical systems exist and evolve within this structure, gravity acts as the universal baseline against which all other interactions are measured. Any theory attempting to unify gravity with the quantum forces must therefore operate at a deeper, pre-geometric level where spacetime itself is not fundamental.}

This principle formalizes the structural asymmetry between gravity and the quantum interactions. It asserts that unification cannot succeed at the level of fields because gravity is not a peer to the gauge forces; it is the metric foundation that makes the gauge forces definable.

\section{Gravity as the Baseline of Measurement}

Gravity defines the fundamental scales of nature:
\begin{align}
\ell_{\mathrm{P}} &= \sqrt{\frac{\hbar G}{c^3}}, \\
t_{\mathrm{P}} &= \sqrt{\frac{\hbar G}{c^5}}, \\
m_{\mathrm{P}} &= \sqrt{\frac{\hbar c}{G}}.
\end{align}

These Planck units set the baseline against which all other physical quantities are measured. Gravity is therefore not merely another field to unify; it is the \emph{metric foundation} of the entire measurement system.

\section{Background Independence}

GR is background independent: the geometry of spacetime is not fixed but determined dynamically. QFT, by contrast, requires a fixed background metric. This incompatibility is central to the failure of quantization attempts \cite{dewitt1967, isham1993}.

\section{Modern Conceptual Insight: Geometry from Quantum Structure}

Recent developments suggest that spacetime geometry may emerge from deeper quantum structure. Entanglement patterns have been shown to encode geometric connectivity in holographic models \cite{maldacena1997, susskind2016}. This motivates the idea that gravity is emergent rather than fundamental.

\section{Implications for Unification}

If gravity is the structure of spacetime, then unification cannot proceed by merging gravity with the quantum forces. Instead, unification must arise from a deeper level where:
\begin{itemize}
    \item spacetime is not fundamental,
    \item gravity emerges from quantum structure,
    \item geometry arises from entanglement or other pre-geometric degrees of freedom.
\end{itemize}

This perspective aligns with emergent spacetime models, holography, and relational approaches to quantum gravity \cite{rovelli2004}.

\section{Conclusion}

GR and QM resist unification because they describe physics at different conceptual levels. Gravity is not a force within spacetime; it is the structure that defines spacetime. All particles and interactions occur within this gravitational framework, making gravity the universal baseline rather than a peer to the quantum forces. A successful unification must therefore arise from a deeper, pre-geometric quantum substrate from which both spacetime and quantum fields emerge.
